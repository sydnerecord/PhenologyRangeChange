This repository contains code for a study that aimed to examine the simultaneous effects of climate change on plant phenology and geographic range shifts across North America, with a focus on two questions. First, are there trade-offs between phenological shifts and geographic range shifts across a broad group of taxa spanning woody to herbaceous species and perennials to annuals? Second, what is the influence of latitude, dispersal mode, and species origin (native vs. non-native) on species phenological and range responses? We used flowering data from herbarium specimens and the National Phenological Network to fit historic and current random forest models of plant and flowering occurrences across the contiguous United States. The results of those models and their associated uncertainties were combined into a spatial Bayesian generalized linear model to examine effects of change in range, dispersal mode, species, species origin, and latitude on change in phenology through time. We found significant effects of geographic range change, the interaction of geographic range change and latitude, and species on phenological change. Overall, our results suggest that climate change is reshaping plant phenological tradeoffs in ways that are contingent on inter-specific variation and geography, but not species origin or dispersal mode. We found support for a tradeoff between phenological shifts and geographic range shifts mediated by latitude. The full citation for the paper will be updated upon publication.
